CASE STUDY 1: GOOGLE MAPS TWO-WHEELER MODE
Author: Raj Arjan
Company: Google
Source: design.google/library/designing-google-maps-motorbikes
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Google Maps developed a dedicated mode for two-wheeler vehicles (motorcycles and
scooters) in India and Indonesia after discovering the platform was designed
exclusively for cars, missing the needs of millions of urban commuters in rapidly
growing cities.

CHALLENGE
In Delhi and Jakarta, traffic congestion had become severe. "In Delhi, the time
people spend in traffic has doubled in the last six years. In Jakarta, the average
is 22 days a year." Two-wheelers represented an essential transportation method
due to their ability to navigate congestion and lower cost compared to cars, yet
Google Maps provided inaccurate routing, arrival times, and navigation features
for these vehicles.

PROCESS

Immersive Field Research:
The team conducted in-person research in Jaipur, Delhi, Bangalore, and Jakarta
with a diverse research group including engineers, UX designers, product managers,
and marketing leads. They rode motorcycles with drivers and observed navigation
methods in real-world conditions.

Key Findings:
- Drivers used varied navigation approaches: memorized routes, voice guidance,
  passenger assistance, or phones mounted via suction devices
- Shortcuts and landmarks significantly improved navigation safety and speed
- Google Maps frequently suggested roads inaccessible to two-wheelers, causing
  frustration
- Drivers operated as communities, relying on each other when technology failed

Community Collaboration:
The team partnered with Go-Jek drivers in Jakarta, conducting workshops to
understand daily routines, community values, and specific pain points. They
created timelines of typical workdays and sketched solutions collaboratively.

Ongoing Testing:
Partners in India and Indonesia continuously tested routing quality, arrival
times, landmark accuracy, and navigation throughout development, identifying
missing roads and landmarks.

SOLUTION
The two-wheeler mode for Google Maps (available on Android in India and
Indonesia) includes:
- Customized routes optimized for two-wheeler accessibility
- Improved voice navigation
- Shortcuts for faster arrival times
- Additional landmarks for better orientation
- Localized terminology ("two-wheeler" for India, "motor" for Indonesia)

Design Principles Approach:
The team established design principles early in development that served as a
foundation for cross-disciplinary collaboration and helped focus on impactful
improvements.

Storytelling Strategy:
Rather than relying solely on data reports, the team captured 360-degree videos,
audio, photographs, and personal stories to convey the emotional and experiential
aspects of two-wheeler navigation. This multimedia approach helped remote team
members connect emotionally with research findings.

RESULTS
The new feature made Google Maps more inclusive for two-wheeler drivers in both
countries. The improvements benefited not just two-wheeler users, but enhanced
the overall experience for everyone using Google Maps in India and Indonesia.
This represented the first new transportation feature added to Google Maps in
years.

KEY QUOTE
"Reading a research report or going to a conference will never offer the same
value as riding on the back of a two-wheeler for 45 minutes, lost because Google
Maps didn't work for you."

METHODOLOGY INSIGHT
Empathy functions as an active practice throughout product development, not merely
a conceptual goal. Direct immersion in user environments yields insights
impossible to obtain through remote research.

CASE STUDY 2: SLACK REDESIGN — DESIGNING TEAMWORK
Authors: Anna Niess & Zack Sultan
Company: Slack
Source: slack.com/blog/designing-the-future-of-slack-with-customers
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Slack underwent a significant design overhaul to address growing complexity. As
the platform expanded with new features, the interface became increasingly
cluttered and non-intuitive for new users.

CHALLENGE
The original interface suffered from information architecture problems:
- The header contained "over a dozen places to click" with duplicate search bars
- Sprawling menus made features difficult to locate
- Even longtime users were unaware of powerful capabilities buried in obscure
  locations
- The accumulated feature additions made it harder to integrate new functionality

PROCESS

Team Assembly:
Slack created a small, cross-functional team of designers, engineers, researchers,
and product managers who developed rough prototypes while stripping away
non-essential interface elements.

Co-Design with Customers:
Rather than relying solely on internal testing, Slack used a shared Slack channel
with approximately 100 users from their champion network across dozens of
organizations worldwide. This approach provided "unfiltered input quickly" and
enabled iterative feedback within days.

Key Learnings from Customers:
- Member count visibility proved critical for "reading the room"
- Users actively sought desired features even when tucked away
- Navigation and history worked best at the top of the app, matching browser-like
  expectations

Testing with New Users:
Slack conducted benchmarking studies comparing old versus new designs. Initial
mixed results led to refinements:
- Floating compose buttons were ignored; top-sidebar placement proved intuitive
- Right-clicking emerged as a primary action, not just a power-user feature
- Collapsible views performed better when opened by default

SOLUTION
- Channel sections for organization
- Simplified header with consolidated search
- Reorganized information architecture
- Improved top navigation bar
- Enhanced right-click menus
- Refined visual themes

RESULTS
The second round of user testing showed the new design "beat or tied the existing
design," providing confidence in improved usability and comprehension for both
new and existing users.

CASE STUDY 3: SLACK ILLUSTRATION SYSTEM
Author: Alice Lee
Company: Slack (via Ueno)
Source: byalicelee.com/slack
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Alice Lee created a comprehensive illustration voice and 40+ asset library for
Slack in 2017, drawing inspiration from animation and publishing rather than the
tech industry to establish a warm, friendly, and distinctive brand identity.

CHALLENGE
Slack needed illustrations that felt warm and approachable while maintaining
sophistication and polish — avoiding generic stock imagery. The work had to
function across diverse contexts: product UI with limited screen space, editorial
content, and various website applications.

PROCESS

Step One — Research & Moodboards:
Lee studied Mary Blair's Disney character design and Charley Harper's "minimal
realist" composition styles. She examined how animation and publishing achieved
warmth through stylized yet grounded characters with minimal linework. Prior
editorial work for Slack's blog served as natural reference points.

Step Two — Two-Part System Development:
The approach separated voice ("how" to draw) from concept ("what" to draw).
Initial explorations tested color palettes and stylization levels, moving from
bright, whimsical tones toward deeper colors (purple, navy) that grounded the
work for an enterprise audience.

Key translated principles:
- Character-driven compositions
- Gestural hand-drawn quality
- Bold primary colors

Step Three — Building the Library:
Working with Slack's internal design team and Ueno designer Gene Ross, Lee
produced 40+ illustrations spanning homepage headers, Slack University materials,
security pages, and UI spot illustrations.

Step Four — Product Design Lens:
Lee created modular components — small spots, blobs, and patterns — for
overlooked contexts like navigation and footers. She adapted illustrations for
mobile screens and considered edge cases across different user flows.

RESULTS
The illustration system launched on slack.com with distinctive character-driven
work that influenced industry trends.

KEY QUOTE
Gene Ross (Ueno Design Lead): "Working with Alice was one of the highlights of my
year — the excitement she brought to the project was matched only by her sheer
talent."

INDUSTRY REFLECTION
Lee distinguishes between two approaches: illustration as stock components versus
intentional brand extensions. She advocates for process-driven methodology that
diversifies references beyond immediate industry peers, drawing from history and
adjacent creative fields.

CASE STUDY 4: OVERCAST 3 — APP REDESIGN
Author: Marco Arment
Company: Overcast (independent)
Source: marco.org/2017/02/20/overcast3
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Marco Arment released Overcast 3, a major redesign of his podcast app after two
years of testing and user feedback. The update fundamentally modernized the
interface while maintaining core functionality.

CHALLENGE
The original Overcast (designed for iOS 7 in 2014) had accumulated several
usability issues:
- Discoverability: Critical features like episode deletion and reordering were
  hidden behind gestures or Edit buttons, generating hundreds of customer emails
  asking how to access existing functionality
- Outdated aesthetic compared to current iOS standards
- Large phone adaptation: Originally designed for iPhone 5S; most users had
  moved to 4.7" and 5.5" screens
- Accidental input: Direct-tap playback caused unintended actions

DESIGN GOALS
1. Update visual style to contemporary standards: thicker fonts, more curves,
   reduced translucency, increased tactility
2. Surface all functionality through visible controls rather than hidden gestures
3. Optimize for larger phones with improved one-handed usability

DESIGN DECISIONS

Card Metaphor Navigation:
Replaced right-slide Now Playing screen with upward-sliding card interface,
matching popular music and podcast apps. Cards dismiss by swiping down anywhere
or using edge-swipe alternatives.

Two-Stage Episode Interaction:
Changed from immediate playback on tap to a selection step revealing action
buttons (Play, Delete, Queue, Info). Though this slows playback, Arment found
it "works far better and more consistently" and users adapt within days.

Visible Reordering Handles:
Replaced hidden Edit-button-activated reordering with permanent drag handles
for better discoverability.

Queue Feature:
Leveraged existing playlist system with special interface placement, allowing
manual or smart playlist options rather than creating a separate queue system.

Episode Info Redesign:
Replaced low-contrast popovers with full-screen cards using familiar gesture
interactions.

Privacy-First Ad Model:
Removed Google's closed-source ad library and analytics, replacing with in-house
advertising featuring podcasts, websites, apps, and premium subscriptions.

SOLUTION
- Horizontal swiping on Now Playing screen reveals effects (left) and episode
  notes (right) with page-dot indicators
- Consolidated Effects and Playback controls into single effects pane
- Multiple podcast subscription from directory without returning between selections
- New widget with rich notifications
- Native watchOS 3 app

RESULTS
- App size grew from 7 MB to approximately 30 MB
- Subscription revenue increased; 90% of revenue from paid subscriptions
- Users typically adapt to two-stage playback within days
- Feature requests for "undiscovered" functionality significantly reduced

KEY QUOTE
"You wouldn't believe how many customers have asked me to add features that were
already there, or couldn't find basic functions like deleting episodes, because
they weren't apparent enough in the design."

CASE STUDY 5: UDACITY MOBILE APP
Author: Aerolab
Company: Udacity
Source: aerolab.co/udacity
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Udacity is an e-learning platform teaching in-demand tech skills. They
commissioned a habit-building tool for students with a reward-based system
centered on motivation through Nanodegrees.

CHALLENGE
The core problem: helping students develop healthy study habits while maintaining
motivation toward complex, long-term educational goals.

PHILOSOPHY
"Help & Motivate" — Rather than focusing on distant objectives, the design
centered on bite-sized steps that would eventually add up to reaching the original
goal. The experience minimized anxiety through gentle reminders and motivational
support.

SOLUTION

Goals & Schedules:
Users selected from four commitment profiles based on availability:
- I'm lazy: < 2 hours/week
- Keep it up: 6 hours/week
- Got time: 12 hours/week
- Hardcore: 24 hours/week

Key Features:
- Divided onboarding for focused attention at each step
- Internal and external notifications with reminders, goal updates, and
  motivational messages
- Visual progress indicators showing evolution toward goals
- Badges and achievements system granting titles to reinforce progress perception

DESIGN IDENTITY
Adapted Udacity's brand to Material Design guidelines with custom icons, logos,
illustrations, and color palette.

Colors: Midnight Knight #2E3D49 / Turquoise Noon #02CCBA / Workaholic Pine
#15C26B / Grey Hour #DBE2E8 / Café Bleu #08B2E2 / Saffron Tea #FFAE0C
Typography: Roboto

NOTES ON CASE STUDY WRITING PATTERNS
Derived from analysis of the above examples
================================================================================

STRUCTURAL PATTERNS
Most strong case studies follow this arc:
  1. Context/Problem Statement — Who are the users? What's broken?
  2. Research/Discovery — What did you learn and how?
  3. Process/Approach — What decisions did you make and why?
  4. Solution — What did you build?
  5. Results/Impact — What changed? (metrics preferred)
  6. Reflection/Learnings — What would you do differently?

VOICE CHARACTERISTICS (observed across top-rated case studies)
- Active voice: "We redesigned..." not "The interface was redesigned..."
- Specific over vague: "80% increase in sign-ups" not "improved conversion"
- Problem-first framing: lead with the user pain, not the feature
- Decision rationale: explain WHY a choice was made, not just what
- Honest about tradeoffs: acknowledge what didn't work or was cut

COMMON WEAKNESSES TO AVOID
- "Users loved it" without supporting evidence
- Process description without showing design decisions
- Listing features instead of explaining impact
- Generic problem statements ("improve UX")
- No connection between research and solution

STRONG OPENING PATTERNS
- State the core tension: "X was designed for Y, but Z needed it"
- Lead with the outcome: "We increased sign-ups by 80%..."
- Start with a human detail: direct quote or observation from research
- Frame the stakes: why does solving this problem matter?

METRICS THAT WORK
- % increase/decrease in specific behaviours (sign-ups, task completion, errors)
- Time savings ("users now complete X in Y seconds vs Z minutes")
- Adoption rates
- Qualitative signals with scale ("hundreds of emails about X stopped")
- NPS or satisfaction score changes
